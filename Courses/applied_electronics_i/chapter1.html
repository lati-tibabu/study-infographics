The World of Semiconductors

From Pure Silicon to the P-N Junction

1. The Atomic Foundation

All electronics are built on materials, and all materials are made of atoms. A semiconductor's unique properties begin with its valence electrons—the outermost electrons that form bonds with other atoms.

Covalent Bonding in Silicon

Silicon (Si), a tetravalent atom, has 4 valence electrons. To become stable, it forms 4 strong covalent bonds with its neighbors, creating a uniform, stable crystal lattice.

2. Classifying Materials by Energy

An electron's ability to move depends on its energy. The Valence Band is where electrons are in bonds. The Conduction Band is where they are free to move. The Energy Gap ($E_g$) between them defines the material.

Semiconductors have a small, non-zero gap, allowing electrons to jump with a moderate amount of energy (like heat), enabling controlled conductivity.

3. The Pure (Intrinsic) Semiconductor

An intrinsic semiconductor is chemically pure, like a perfect silicon crystal. At room temperature, thermal energy is just enough to break some covalent bonds, creating mobile charge carriers.

When an electron jumps to the conduction band, it leaves a vacancy called a hole. This hole acts as a positive charge carrier. In pure material, Electrons = Holes.

Two Types of Current

Drift Current

The movement of carriers caused by an applied Electric Field. Electrons move one way, holes move the other.

Diffusion Current

The natural movement of carriers from a region of High Concentration to one of Low Concentration.

4. Engineering Conductivity (Extrinsic)

We rarely use pure semiconductors. Instead, we perform Doping—intentionally adding impurities to create an excess of one type of charge carrier.

5. The P-N Junction: The Diode's Core

The most basic electronic component is formed by joining P-type and N-type material. The interaction at this boundary is the key to all modern electronics.

Electrons diffuse from N to P, and holes from P to N. They recombine near the junction, leaving behind fixed ions. This area, empty of mobile carriers, is the Depletion Region.

The Built-in Potential Barrier

The fixed ions create an internal electric field, which in turn creates a voltage barrier. This barrier opposes further diffusion and establishes equilibrium.
